The History

France was originally part of the Roman Empire. However, it was conquered by a Germanic tribe known as the Franks in the late 5th century AD.The original inhabitants of France were a Celtic people known as the Gauls. Later inhabitants included the Romans and the Franks.France was originally called Gaul during Roman times. It was also called Frankia when it was first conquered by the Franks in the 5th century AD.France is known for many things in history, including being the center of European culture for centuries. It is also known for the French Revolution, the Enlightenment, and the birthplace of the French language.Clovis I, king of the Franks, is generally considered to be the founder of the French nation. It is from the Franks that France gets its name.

The history behind the flag

The "tricolore" (three-colour) flag is an emblem of the Fifth Republic. It had its origins in the union, at the time of the French Revolution, of the colours of the King (white) and the City of Paris (blue and red). Today, the "tricolour" flies over all public buildings.Why does France have 2 flags? Since 1976, there have been two versions of the flag in varying levels of use by the state: the original (identifiable by its use of navy blue) and one with a lighter shade of blue. Since 2021, France has used the older variant by default, including at the Élysée Palac.

Places to go see:

  1. The Eiffel Tower

    2. The Eiffel Tower was built in 1889 by engineer Gustave Eiffel as the entrance arch for the World’s Fair. The Eiffel Tower is made of iron and is the tallest structure in Paris. It is one of the most recognisable landmarks in the world.The Eiffel Tower was built in 1889 and is one of the most iconic landmarks in Paris.

  2. Louvre Museum

    3. The Louvre is a world-famous art museum in Paris, France. It is one of the largest museums in the world, and contains a large collection of art from a variety of periods and cultures.The Louvre was originally built as a fortress in the 12th century. It became a royal palace in the 14th century, and was transformed into a museum in the 17th century.While there you can see the Mona Lisa, the Venus de Milo, and the Nike of Samothrace are some of the most famous works of art at the Louvre.

  3. Arc de Triomphe

    4. This awe-inspiring monument is one of the most iconic sights in all of France, and it’s well worth the trip to see it.The Arc de Triomphe is a gigantic arch that was built to commemorate the victory of France over Austria and Prussia in the Battle of Triomphe in 1815. The arch is made of marble and is 72 meters high, 61 meters wide, and 23 meters deep.The arch is located in the center of the famous Champs-Elysées avenue in Paris, and it is a popular tourist destination. Visitors can walk through the arch, or they can take the elevator up to the top.

  4. Pont Alexandre III

    5. Pont Alexandre III This ornate, Beaux-Arts–style bridge connects the Left and Right Banks of Paris, and is one of the biggest tourist photo-ops in the city. We get it: With all those gilded statues and river views, the selfie urge is real.

  5. Disneyland Park in Paris

    6. Disneyland Paris is a theme park in Marne-la-Vallee, France. The park opened on April 12, 1992 as Euro Disneyland, and is the most visited theme park in all of Europe.Disneyland Paris is about 20 miles from Paris. With two themeparks, dozens of rides, and countless characters to meet, there’s something for everyone at Disneyland Paris. In addition to the themeparks, Disneyland Paris also has a variety of restaurants, shops, and shows to enjoy.

Food That Paris Has to Offer:

  • Macarons

    • These small, colorful cookies are one of Paris’ most popular sweet treats. Made of meringue, almond flour, and sugar, they have a crunchy exterior and are filled with buttercream, jam, or ganache. They come in a variety of flavors, such as coffee, pistachio, raspberry, caramel, chocolate, and vanilla.

  • Croissants

    • It’s a common fact that no Parisian breakfast would be complete without a croissant (or two we’re not judging). But despite the abundance of bakeries in the city, not all of them are created equal. And when it comes to pastries in France, you should only settle for the best of the best.

  • Crepes

    • Similar to a very thin pancake, the flexible crepe can be breakfast, savory, flambéed with Grand Marnier, or served as dessert with a scoop of ice cream.One's breakfast could not be full without this wonderful treat.

  • Freshly Baked Bread

    • Anywhere within the confines of Paris’ Peripherique, a boulangerie (bakery) is no more than a 6-minute walk away. Bread is the main building block of French food and it is delicious. Fresh, quality bread can be found almost anywhere.
